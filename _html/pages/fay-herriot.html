Area-Level SAE Models

Area-level SAE models are usually the first model-based step after direct estimation. They are useful when direct estimates exist for at least some domains but are too noisy, unstable, or missing for others.

The standard starting point is the Fay-Herriot (FH) model. It does not model individual survey records. Instead, it models domain-level direct estimates and their sampling variances.

1 When Fay-Herriot Is Useful

Use an area-level model when:

  • You have direct estimates for sampled domains.
  • You have an estimated sampling variance for each direct estimate.
  • You have domain-level covariates for every domain you want to estimate.
  • Unit-level population microdata are unavailable, restricted, or not comparable with the survey.
  • The target output is one estimate per area.

FH is particularly attractive for surveys where the public or project-safe data can be prepared as one row per domain.

2 Required Inputs

For each domain (d=1,,D), the FH model needs:

  • (^{DIR}_d): direct estimate from the survey.
  • (_d): estimated sampling variance of the direct estimate.
  • (x_d): a vector of area-level covariates.
  • A domain identifier that aligns across survey, covariate, and boundary files.

The model is usually fitted on domains with usable direct estimates and variances. Predictions can then be produced for unsampled domains using the covariates and model structure.

3 Two-Stage Model

The FH model is often described as two linked models.

3.1 Sampling Model

The direct estimate is treated as a noisy observation of the true domain value:

\[ \hat{\theta}^{DIR}_d = \theta_d + e_d, \]

where:

\[ e_d \sim N(0, \psi_d). \]

Here, (_d) is the sampling variance estimated during direct estimation. The model treats (_d) as known, even though in practice it is estimated.

3.2 Linking Model

The true domain value is related to area-level covariates:

\[ \theta_d = x_d^\top\beta + u_d, \]

where:

\[ u_d \sim N(0, \sigma_u^2). \]

The random effect (u_d) captures between-area variation not explained by the covariates.

Combining both pieces gives:

\[ \hat{\theta}^{DIR}_d = x_d^\top\beta + u_d + e_d. \]

4 Shrinkage Interpretation

The FH estimate can be written as a weighted average of:

  • the direct estimate (^{DIR}_d), and
  • the regression-synthetic estimate (x_d^).

The empirical best linear unbiased predictor (EBLUP) is:

\[ \hat{\theta}^{FH}_d = \hat{\gamma}_d\hat{\theta}^{DIR}_d + (1-\hat{\gamma}_d)x_d^\top\hat{\beta}. \]

The shrinkage factor is:

\[ \hat{\gamma}_d = \frac{\hat{\sigma}_u^2} {\hat{\sigma}_u^2+\psi_d}. \]

Interpretation:

  • If (_d) is small, the direct estimate is precise and (_d) is close to 1.
  • If (_d) is large, the direct estimate is noisy and (_d) is closer to 0.
  • Unsampled domains have no direct estimate, so their estimates rely on the synthetic component and any modelled area effect structure.

This is why FH estimates should usually stay close to precise direct estimates but move noisy direct estimates toward the model prediction.

5 Estimating Model Uncertainty

FH uncertainty is usually summarised through an estimated mean squared error:

\[ \operatorname{MSE}\left(\hat{\theta}^{FH}_d\right) = E\left[ \left( \hat{\theta}^{FH}_d-\theta_d \right)^2 \right]. \]

In practice, packages such as sae return an MSE estimate for the EBLUP. The square root of the MSE is often reported as an RMSE or model standard error.

Be careful: FH MSE is model-based. It is not the same object as the sampling variance of a direct estimate.

6 Baseline sae Pattern

library(dplyr)
library(sae)

fh_data <- direct_estimates |>
  left_join(covariates, by = "domain_id")

fh_fit <- eblupFH(
  direct ~ prop_no_car + prop_urban + median_age,
  vardir = variance,
  data = fh_data
)

fh_results <- fh_data |>
  mutate(
    fh_estimate = fh_fit$eblup$eblup,
    fh_mse = fh_fit$mse$mse,
    fh_rmse = sqrt(fh_mse)
  )

7 Covariate Choice

Use covariates that are:

  • available for every target domain,
  • measured at the correct geography,
  • plausibly related to the indicator,
  • comparable in time to the survey,
  • not too collinear with each other,
  • interpretable enough to explain to users.

For transport and energy indicators, useful covariate families might include age structure, household composition, car availability, urban-rural classification, housing tenure, employment, deprivation, public transport access, and energy-related housing characteristics.

8 Practical Checks

Before trusting the model, check:

  • Are direct estimates and variances plausible?
  • Are zero or missing sampling variances handled?
  • Are covariate signs and magnitudes plausible?
  • Are predictions outside the valid range for proportions?
  • Do FH estimates stay close to precise direct estimates?
  • Which areas are strongly pulled toward the model?
  • Do residuals show remaining spatial pattern?

9 Limitations

FH is often practical, but it has important limitations:

  • It assumes the direct estimate is approximately normal.
  • It treats estimated sampling variances as known.
  • It uses one observation per domain, so the number of model observations can be small.
  • Each indicator usually needs a separate model.
  • It cannot produce sub-domain estimates unless a new model is built at that geography.
  • For proportions near 0 or 1, a transformation or alternative model may be needed.

10 Transformations for Proportions

For binary indicators, plain FH can produce estimates below 0 or above 1. This is most likely when direct estimates are near the boundary or sampling variances are large.

Possible responses:

  • Use a logit or arcsine transformation.
  • Fit a binomial or beta-binomial model where appropriate.
  • Treat boundary estimates and zero variances carefully before modelling.
  • Report when transformed estimates have been back-transformed.

The right choice depends on the indicator, sample size, and modelling package.

11 What to Report

At minimum, report:

  • direct estimate,
  • direct sampling variance or standard error,
  • FH estimate,
  • FH MSE or RMSE,
  • sample size or non-missing sample size,
  • covariates used,
  • areas where model estimates differ strongly from direct estimates.
