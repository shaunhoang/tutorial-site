Area-Level Estimation (Bayesian) + Spatial

This notebook extends the area-level Fay-Herriot workflow. It first reframes area-level SAE in Bayesian terms, then shows why spatial structure may be useful when neighbouring domains share unexplained patterns.

1 Bayesian versus Frequentist approach

Both frequentist and Bayesian area-level SAE start from the same basic Fay-Herriot idea that the domain direct estimates are :

\[ \hat{\theta}^{DIR}_d = x_d^\top\beta + u_d + e_d. \]

The difference is how the unknown quantities are estimated and how uncertainty is interpreted.

  • In the frequentist approach, unknown quantities such as \(\beta\), \(\sigma_u^2\), and \(u_d\) are estimated as fixed unknowns. Typical outputs are one point estimate per area (EBLUP), uncertainty measures such as RMSE, and confidence intervals.
  • In the Bayesian approach, these unknown quantities are instead treated as uncertain and assigned prior distributions. Typical outputs are posterior means, credible intervals, and probability summaries.

This means that, with a Bayesian approach, each area has a distribution of plausible values. This makes it natural to report a central estimate, an uncertainty interval, and probability-style summaries, such as how likely an area is to be above a certain policy threshold.

Therefore, in practice, there is little need to perform Area-Level SAE using both approaches sequentially, especially when there are suitable R packages that have simplified the more complex implementation of the Bayesian approach.

2 Spatial Structure

Going back to the FH model equation cited above, the non-spatial area-level model assumes the unexplained area effects \(u_d\) are independent after accounting for covariates. In urban research, this is rarely true. This extension of the Fay-Herriot model assumes spatial correlation between areas, i.e., the values of one area will be influenced by the values of the areas around it.

\[ u = \rho W u + \eta,\qquad \eta \sim N(0,\sigma^2 I) \]

Adding spatial structure, usually a binary spatial weights matrix \(W\), lets the model borrow strength not only from covariates, but also from nearby areas.

3 Code Implementation

Tip

For Bayesian area-level SAE, the main package used here is SUMMER. It fits area-level smoothing models using INLA, and can work either from direct estimates or from survey microdata, greatly simplifying the implementation of the Bayesian approach and adding spatial structure in Area-Level SAE.

  • Use SUMMER::smoothArea() when you already have direct estimates and their variances.
  • Use SUMMER::smoothSurvey() when you want SUMMER to calculate design-adjusted direct estimates from survey microdata before smoothing.

The sae package, which applies a Frequentist approach to SAE, does not handle out-of-sample domains (i.e., domains without respondent records) and spatial structure automatically. These cases require extra implementation work.
