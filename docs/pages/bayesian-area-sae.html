[WIP] Area-Level Estimation (Bayesian)

Area-level SAE improves direct estimates that are too noisy, unstable, or unavailable for some domains. The standard starting point is the Fay-Herriot (FH) model, which combines direct survey estimates with domain-level covariates to improve precision.

1 Understanding the basic Fay-Herriot model

The Fay-Herriot model is an area-level small area estimation model. It combines two sources of information for each domain:

  1. the direct survey estimate for the domain, and
  2. the model-based prediction from covariates.

Their relationship can be written as:

\[ \hat{\theta}^{DIR}_d = x_d^\top \beta + u_d + e_d \]

where:

  • \(\hat{\theta}^{DIR}_d\) is the direct survey estimate for domain \(d\);
  • \(x_d^\top \beta\) is the part explained by covariates (i.e., model prediction);
  • \(u_d\) is the domain-specific unexplained effect;
  • \(e_d\) is the survey sampling error.

The Fay-Herriot estimate is then a weighted average between the direct estimate and the model-based prediction:

\[ \tilde{\theta}^{FH}_d = \gamma_d \hat{\theta}^{DIR}_d + (1-\gamma_d)x_d^\top\hat{\beta} \]

The shrinkage weight \(\gamma_d =\frac{\sigma_u^2}{\sigma_u^2 + \psi_d}\) controls how much the final estimate relies on the direct survey estimate versus the model-based prediction, using sampling variance \(\psi_d\) and between-domain unexplained variance \(\sigma_u^2\) (i.e., variance of \(u_d\)).

  • If \(\psi_d\) is small (the direct estimate is precise), \(\gamma_d\) is closer to 1 and the FH estimate stays close to the direct estimate;
  • If \(\psi_d\) is large (the direct estimate is noisy), \(\gamma_d\) is closer to 0 and the FH estimate is pulled more strongly toward the model-based prediction;
  • If \(\sigma_u^2\) is large (strong remaining differences among domains), the FH estimate gives more weight to the direct estimate.
  • If \(\sigma_u^2\) is small (weak remaining differences among domains), the FH estimate is pulled more strongly toward the model-based prediction.

Essentially, the Fay-Herriot model evaluates how reliable the direct estimates are. Reliable direct estimates are mostly preserved, while noisy direct estimates borrow more information from the model.

2 What is the Bayesian approach?

The Bayesian approach treats the unknown quantities as uncertain. Instead of estimating \(\beta\), \(\sigma_u^2\), and \(\gamma_d\) once and plugging them into the formula, the Bayesian model gives them probability distributions.

As a result, the Bayesian FH estimate is usually reported as the posterior mean of a distribution of plausible true values with a credible interval.

E.g., a 95% credible interval represents the range within which the true domain value lies with 95% probability.

The Bayesian approach is also flexible when dealing with domains with little or no survey data. If a domain has no direct estimate, the model can still predict a plausible value using its covariates and the patterns learned from other domains. That said, these estimates will be by design more model-dependent and usually have wider credible intervals.

3 Spatial structure

The basic Fay-Herriot model usually assumes that the unexplained area effects \(u_d\) are independent after accounting for covariates. In urban research, this assumption is often unrealistic. Nearby areas may share infrastructure, socio-demographic profiles, transport conditions, etc. As a result, neighbouring areas may still have similar unexplained effects even after covariates have been included.

A spatial Fay-Herriot model relaxes this independence assumption by allowing the unexplained area effects to be spatially related. One way to express this is:

\[ u = \rho W u + \eta \]

where:

  • \(u\) is the vector of unexplained area effects;
  • \(W\) is a spatial weights matrix describing which areas are neighbours (based on adjacency, distance, etc.);
  • \(\rho\) controls the strength of spatial dependence;
  • \(\eta\) is the remaining non-spatial random variation.

[ADD IMAGE - spatial weight matrix]

Essentially, the model informed by \(W\) tries to relate the unexplained effect in one area to the unexplained effects in nearby areas. The strength of this spatial relationship is estimated by the model:

  • if nearby areas have similar unexplained effects, \(\rho\) will indicate stronger spatial dependence, and the model applies more spatial smoothing;
  • if nearby areas are not strongly related, the spatial effect contributes less, and the model behaves more like the non-spatial Fay-Herriot model.

4 Code Implementation

Tip

For Bayesian area-level SAE, the main package used here is SUMMER. It fits area-level smoothing models using INLA, and can work either from direct estimates or from survey microdata, greatly simplifying the implementation of the Bayesian approach and adding spatial structure in Area-Level SAE.

  • Use SUMMER::smoothArea() when you already have direct estimates and their variances.
  • Use SUMMER::smoothSurvey() when you want SUMMER to calculate design-adjusted direct estimates from survey microdata before smoothing.
