Overview

Raw survey microdata is transformed into analysis-ready inputs by defining the estimand, coding the indicator from the survey variables, aligning it with the estimation geography, and choosing a compatible survey weight.

The data preparation pipeline can be summarised as follows:

1 Define the Estimands

The estimand determines how survey microdata inputs are prepared and how to choose the most appropriate estimation approach downstream.

E.g.,

  1. Share of adults who use the train weekly by MSOA
  2. Mean leisure travel distance among retirees by Ward

The composition of a well-formed estimand is:

Estimate the measure of what outcome, among which population, across which geography.

WE will take a closer look at each of these components and the implications on the methodology.

1.1 Estimation geography

In SAE, the groups or areas being estimated are often called domains. In the spatial context such as urban and transport research, they are better understood as estimation areas instead. An estimation area is one unit in the chosen estimation geography. In the UK, they can be one Local Authority, Ward, LSOA, or MSOA.

The chosen estimation geography must match the level of spatial detail available in the survey data. For example, if respondents are identified only by Local Authority, then Local Authority is the finest geography that can be used for small-area estimation. SAE methods are not intended to “upsample” survey data to a more granular spatial resolution.

1.2 Measure & Outcome

Based on the estimands, identify or create the survey indicator that represents the outcome and how they should be aggregated for each estimation area, by using resources such as the survey data user guide, survey questionnaire, response codes, and routing rules to guide indicator construction. Some indicators are direct recodes of one survey question; others need to combine several variables. The same raw survey variable can support different estimands.

The most common types of outcome indicator are:

Indicator type What to define Example
Binary What is 1, 0, missing, and out of scope. Also suitable for categorical variables Estimand: Share of adults who use the train weekly
Indicator: Uses train weekly: yes/no
Continuous The numeric value and unit Estimand: Mean leisure travel distance among retirees
Indicator: Leisure travel distance in miles

The most common choices of measure, i.e., how the outcome indicators are aggregated, are:

  • share (e.g., % of EV among car owners)
  • mean (e.g., average trip duration)
  • rate in population (e.g., accidents per 1,000)
  • count/total (uncommon)

2 Weights and domain sizes

Survey weights connect the sample back to the population to ensure the survey sample can best represent real-world proportions. The survey user guide is the go-to resource to understand which weight variables are available, what they adjust for, and most importantly, which survey modules/questions they apply to. In general, weight variables provided by surveys have already been derived from one or more adjustments, such as:

  1. Selection adjustment: accounts for unequal probabilities of selection.
  2. Non-response adjustment: compensates for differences between respondents and non-respondents.
  3. Calibration adjustment: aligns the weighted sample with known population totals, such as age, sex, region, Local Authority, tenure, or household structure.
  4. Subsample adjustment: accounts for cases where only part of the sample was asked a module or completed a diary.

For SAE, there is no one-size-fits-all approach to survey weight selection for the estimand. The weight used must match the estimand unit, target population, and survey design. Moreover, it is important to distinguish the scale of the weight variable:

  • Analysis weights \(w^{A}\) make means or proportions representative of the target population, and they generally sum to the sample size. These weights are most common for estimating share or mean.
    • E.g., if a sampling unit has an analysis weight of 0.5, it is over-represented in the sample relative to the population, so its contribution is ‘down-weighted’.
  • Grossing weights \(w^{G}\) are essentially upscaled-\(w^{A}\) so that they sum to a known population total within the relevant estimation areas. They are usually required if the estimand measure is count, or rate.
    • E.g., if a sampling unit has a grossing weight of 50, it means this one sampled row represents about 50 people in the population.

In SAE, domain size \(N_d\) defines the size of the target population in each estimation area, or domain. For example, if the estimation geography is Local Authority and the target population is adults aged 16+, then \(N_d\) is the number of adults aged 16+ in each Local Authority. Domain sizes are usually obtained from Census data, population estimates, administrative data, or other regional statistics. The correct domain size must match both the target population and the estimation geography used in the estimand.

They can be used to scale analysis weights into grossing weights in order to support estimands that depend on the actual number of people, households, or units in an area. For example:

  • If estimating the number of households who struggle with transport costs by Ward, \(N_d\) would be the total household count in each ward. It is used to scale sample-based weights and estimates into a population-level ones.
  • If estimating a claimant rate per 1,000 working-age residents by MSOA, \(N_d\) would be the working-age population in each MSOA, used as the denominator.

For share or mean estimands, SAE is possible even when domain sizes are unknown, however with certain nuances in interpretation.

Tip

If analysis weights are provided and the relevant domain sizes are known, they can be rescaled into grossing weights for each survey respondent:

\[ w^G_{di} = w^A_{di} \times \frac{N_d}{\sum_{i \in d} w^A_{di}} \]

where \(w^A_{di}\) is the analysis weight for sampled unit \(i\) in domain \(d\), \(w^G_{di}\) is the corresponding grossing weight, and \(N_d\) is that domain’s size.

3 Select covariates

SAE improves precision of the estimates by “borrowing strength” from auxiliary variables (i.e., covariates). This is critical for estimation areas with sparse or no survey data. Covariates can often be found via census data and other population-level aggregate statistics. Choose covariates with emphasis on coverage and comparability across estimation areas.

The key attributes of good covariates are:

  • Coverage: each covariate should be available for all estimation areas.
  • Alignment: match geography, definition, and reference period to the survey indicator.
  • Relevance: keep variables with a clear substantive link to the outcome and avoid including many weak predictors.
  • Predictive: choose covariates that improve area-level prediction and are easy to explain.

For example, some common covariates when estimating transport-related indicators are: age, income, employment profile, population density, housing mix, deprivation, urban/rural classification, transport accessibility metrics, car ownership, etc.

4 Preparation Summary

Putting everything together, each of the estimands of interest should have a short record as follows that will guide how we prepare input datasets for the SAE workflow. For example, a compact record could be written as:

# Estimand Indicator Variable coding rule Measure Estimation geography Weight variable Covariates
1 Share of adults who use the train weekly across MSOAs is_freq_train_user Binary: 1 if TrainUseFreq = 1; 0 if adult does not use the train weekly; null for invalid or refused responses Share MSOA SampleAdultWeight age,income,own_carvan, etc.
2 Mean leisure travel distance among retirees across Wards leisure_travel_distance Continuous: numeric miles from TravelWorkDistance; only retirees with valid leisure travel distance are in scope Mean Ward LeisureTravelModuleWeight income,own_home,own_carvan, etc.
