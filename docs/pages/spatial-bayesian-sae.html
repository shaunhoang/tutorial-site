Bayesian Area and Spatial Smoothing

Spatial SAE adds structure when neighbouring domains are expected to have similar values after accounting for covariates. Bayesian SAE extends this by representing uncertainty through posterior distributions.

1 When spatial structure helps

Spatial modelling is most defensible when:

  • The indicator has a plausible spatial process.
  • Domains are geographically meaningful for the process.
  • Residuals from a non-spatial model show spatial patterning.
  • The adjacency structure is reliable and aligned with the modelling table.

2 When to be cautious

Spatial smoothing can hide real local differences. It can also create overconfident maps if adjacency is treated as a substitute for missing survey signal.

3 Adjacency checks

library(sf)
library(spdep)

domains_sf <- st_read("../data/boundaries/boundaries-ltla.geojson")

neighbors <- poly2nb(domains_sf, queen = TRUE)
adjacency <- nb2mat(neighbors, style = "B", zero.policy = TRUE)

stopifnot(nrow(adjacency) == nrow(domains_sf))

This section should introduce the practical pattern for SUMMER and INLA:

  1. Prepare direct estimates and variances.
  2. Prepare domain covariates.
  3. Prepare an adjacency graph.
  4. Fit a non-spatial Bayesian FH model.
  5. Fit a spatial FH model.
  6. Compare posterior summaries, residuals, and mapped uncertainty.
