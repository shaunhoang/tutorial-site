Unit-Level SAE Models

Unit-level SAE models use survey microdata directly. They can be more powerful than area-level models, but they require much stronger data conditions.

For this TRACK-UK tutorial, unit-level SAE is mainly included as theory and context. The practical workflow focuses on area-level models because the available survey and covariate structure is better suited to Fay-Herriot-style estimation.

1 Area-Level versus Unit-Level SAE

Feature Area-level SAE Unit-level SAE
Main data input Direct estimates by domain Individual or household survey records
Auxiliary data Domain-level covariates Unit-level covariates for the population or poststrata
Typical model Fay-Herriot Nested-error model, GLMM, MRP, empirical best prediction
Output One estimate per area Estimates can be aggregated to several geographies
Practical burden Lower Higher
Main risk Too little information per domain Survey-population mismatch and stronger model assumptions

2 Basic Unit-Level Idea

Suppose (y_{di}) is the outcome for unit (i) in domain (d), and (x_{di}) is a vector of unit-level covariates.

A common nested-error model is:

\[ y_{di} = x_{di}^{\top}\beta + u_d + e_{di}, \]

where:

\[ u_d \sim N(0, \sigma_u^2), \quad e_{di} \sim N(0, \sigma_e^2). \]

The domain random effect (u_d) captures unexplained differences between areas. The unit error (e_{di}) captures unexplained differences between people, households, or other sampled units within areas.

For binary outcomes, the model is usually changed to a logistic or probit mixed model:

\[ \operatorname{logit}(p_{di}) = x_{di}^{\top}\beta + u_d, \]

where:

\[ y_{di} \sim \operatorname{Bernoulli}(p_{di}). \]

3 Estimating a Domain Mean

The finite-population target for domain (d) is:

\[ \theta_d = \frac{1}{N_d} \sum_{i \in U_d} y_{di}. \]

The sampled units have observed (y_{di}). For the non-sampled population units, the model predicts or simulates (y_{di}) using their covariates.

A simple empirical best prediction idea is:

\[ \hat{\theta}^{EBP}_d = \frac{1}{N_d} \left[ \sum_{i \in s_d} y_{di} + \sum_{i \in r_d} \hat{y}_{di} \right], \]

where (r_d) is the non-sampled part of the population in domain (d). In practice, many methods simulate values for the non-sampled population repeatedly, then summarise the simulated domain estimates.

4 Required Data

Unit-level SAE usually needs:

  • survey microdata with the outcome and unit-level covariates,
  • population or census microdata with the same covariates for all units, or poststratification cell counts,
  • comparable variable definitions across survey and population data,
  • domain identifiers that link the survey and population data,
  • weights or design information, especially if the survey design is informative,
  • enough sampled domains to estimate area effects reliably.

The most difficult condition is comparability. If a covariate is measured differently in the survey and census, or if the survey and census refer to different populations or periods, the resulting estimates can be biased.

5 Why It Can Be Powerful

Unit-level models can:

  • use more information than area-level models,
  • estimate indicators for unsampled domains,
  • produce estimates at multiple aggregation levels,
  • handle non-linear indicators through simulation,
  • use individual or household covariates rather than area averages.

This is why unit-level methods are common in poverty mapping and microsimulation contexts.

6 Why It Is Not the Main Practical Route Here

For the current TRACK-UK SAE tutorial, area-level models are more productive because:

  • the worked survey outputs are naturally prepared as indicator-by-domain files,
  • Local Authority identifiers are available for the National Survey for Wales example,
  • population microdata with all required matching covariates may not be available,
  • routed transport indicators can make unit-level target populations difficult to define,
  • the aim is to teach a reusable workflow for researchers starting from direct estimates.

Unit-level SAE is still useful to understand. It becomes more relevant if the project later connects SAE with synthetic populations or poststratification cell counts.

7 Relationship to Synthetic Populations

Synthetic populations and unit-level SAE can work together. A synthetic population can provide a population-like set of units with covariates, and a fitted survey model can predict or simulate outcomes for those units.

However, this introduces additional assumptions:

  • the synthetic population must represent the target domains well,
  • survey and synthetic-population covariates must be comparable,
  • uncertainty from the synthetic population should not be ignored,
  • model predictions should be validated against direct estimates or known aggregates where possible.

The connection is noted here, but a full synthetic population SAE workflow is outside the present scope.

8 Practical Pattern

A generic unit-level workflow is:

  1. Harmonise survey and population covariates.
  2. Fit a mixed model to the survey microdata.
  3. Predict or simulate outcomes for the non-sampled population.
  4. Aggregate predicted and observed outcomes to domains.
  5. Estimate uncertainty using bootstrap, posterior simulation, or model-specific MSE methods.
  6. Compare estimates against direct estimates and higher-level benchmarks.

9 Caveats

  • Unit-level SAE is model-heavy and should be checked carefully.
  • If the survey design is informative, ignoring weights can bias estimates.
  • If population covariates are weak predictors, estimates can be poor even with many population records.
  • MSE estimation can be computationally expensive.
  • Outliers, non-normality, and model misspecification can matter.
  • The method can give a false sense of detail if the population microdata are synthetic or weakly validated.

10 Notes on Sources

The standard unit-level SAE idea is to model the outcome using survey microdata, then predict or simulate outcomes for population units before aggregating to domains. See the unit-level SAE chapter in the poverty mapping guide for a fuller discussion of empirical best prediction, census-based workflows, requirements, and caveats.
